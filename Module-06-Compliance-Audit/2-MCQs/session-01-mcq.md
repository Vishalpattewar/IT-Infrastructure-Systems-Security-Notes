## MCQ Set

---

**Q1. What is the primary purpose of a Compliance Audit?**

A) Identify and exploit vulnerabilities in a system
B) Monitor real-time network traffic for active threats
C) Verify that an organization adheres to defined standards and regulations ✅
D) Test the effectiveness of penetration testing tools

> **Explanation:** A compliance audit is a systematic review to verify
> **adherence** to standards, laws, or internal policies — it is not
> vulnerability hunting (that is pen testing). A describes pen testing.
> B describes network monitoring. D describes tool evaluation.

---

**Q2. An organization has passed its annual compliance audit. This means the
organization is completely secure. This statement is:**

A) True — compliance audit verifies all security controls are working
B) True — external auditors validate complete security posture
C) False — compliance is a minimum baseline, not a security guarantee ✅
D) False — only internal audits can guarantee security

> **Explanation:** **Compliance ≠ Security.** Passing a compliance audit means
> you meet a defined set of minimum requirements. Real-world threats can still
> exploit gaps not covered by those requirements. Neither internal nor external
> audits guarantee complete security.

---

**Q3. Which of the following best describes "Shadow IT"?**

A) IT systems used by external attackers
B) Backup systems kept offline for disaster recovery
C) Unauthorized systems or apps used within an organization without IT approval ✅
D) Legacy systems that are no longer supported by vendors

> **Explanation:** **Shadow IT** = employees or departments using unauthorized
> apps or systems without IT knowledge or approval. A describes attacker
> infrastructure. B describes DR systems. D describes legacy systems — a
> separate challenge entirely.

---

**Q4. Which type of security audit is specifically conducted to investigate
fraud or a security incident?**

A) Operational Audit
B) Technical Audit
C) Compliance Audit
D) Forensic Audit ✅

> **Explanation:** **Forensic Audit** is investigative — used specifically for
> fraud detection or post-incident investigation. Technical audit reviews
> technical controls. Operational reviews process efficiency. Compliance checks
> standards adherence.

---

**Q5. An audit conducted by a customer on its supplier or vendor is formally
classified as:**

A) First Party Audit
B) Second Party Audit ✅
C) Third Party Audit
D) External Audit

> **Explanation:** Per **ISO 19011**, a **Second Party Audit** is when a
> customer audits its own supplier or vendor. First Party = organization audits
> itself. Third Party = independent certification body or regulator. External
> audit is a general term — Second Party is the precise ISO classification.

---

**Q6. What is the correct sequence of Security Audit Phases?**

A) Fieldwork → Planning → Analysis → Reporting → Follow-up
B) Planning → Analysis → Fieldwork → Reporting → Follow-up
C) Planning → Fieldwork → Reporting → Analysis → Follow-up
D) Planning → Fieldwork → Analysis → Reporting → Follow-up ✅

> **Explanation:** The correct order is **Planning** (define scope) →
> **Fieldwork** (collect evidence) → **Analysis** (evaluate findings) →
> **Reporting** (document results) → **Follow-up** (verify fixes). B swaps
> Analysis and Fieldwork. C swaps Reporting and Analysis. A starts with
> Fieldwork which skips Planning entirely.

---

**Q7. Which audit phase is most commonly skipped in practice but is critical
for audit effectiveness?**

A) Planning
B) Fieldwork
C) Follow-up ✅
D) Reporting

> **Explanation:** **Follow-up** verifies that corrective actions were actually
> implemented after the audit report. Without it, findings are documented but
> never fixed. It is the most overlooked phase and a very common MCQ trap.

---

**Q8. The single most critical requirement for an internal audit team is:**

A) Access to the latest security tools and scanners
B) Large team size for broader coverage
C) Independence from the departments being audited ✅
D) Physical proximity to the systems being audited

> **Explanation:** **Independence** is non-negotiable — if auditors report to
> or are part of the departments they audit, objectivity is compromised and
> findings can be suppressed. Size, tools, and location are secondary
> considerations.

---

**Q9. The internal audit team's reporting line should go directly to:**

A) The Chief Information Officer (CIO)
B) The Chief Technology Officer (CTO)
C) The IT Manager
D) The Board or Audit Committee ✅

> **Explanation:** Reporting to the **Board or Audit Committee** (not
> operational management) preserves independence. Reporting to CIO, CTO, or IT
> Manager creates a conflict of interest since those functions may themselves
> be subjects of an audit.

---

**Q10. Which document formally defines the internal audit function's purpose,
authority, and responsibility?**

A) Audit Report
B) Audit Plan
C) Audit Charter ✅
D) Audit Scope Statement

> **Explanation:** The **Audit Charter** is the formal document that defines
> the audit function's authority, purpose, and responsibility. It is approved
> by the Board or Audit Committee — not management. Without it, auditors have
> no formal authority to request access to systems or records.

---

**Q11. The principle that requires auditors to base conclusions only on
verifiable, documented evidence is called:**

A) Independence
B) Confidentiality
C) Evidence-based Approach ✅
D) Risk-based Approach

> **Explanation:** The **Evidence-based Approach** principle means all
> conclusions must come from verifiable evidence — not assumptions or verbal
> assurances. Independence is about freedom from conflicts. Confidentiality is
> about protecting audit information. Risk-based is about prioritizing by risk.

---

**Q12. An auditor who questions all claims and seeks evidence rather than
accepting verbal assurances is demonstrating:**

A) Bias against the auditee
B) Professional Skepticism ✅
C) Overreach beyond audit scope
D) Lack of communication skills

> **Explanation:** **Professional Skepticism** is a required and respected
> auditor quality — it means maintaining a questioning mindset and seeking
> verifiable evidence rather than accepting statements at face value. It is not
> bias or overreach; it is a defined professional standard.

---

**Q13. An IDS (Intrusion Detection System) is an example of which type of
security control?**

A) Preventive Control
B) Compensating Control
C) Corrective Control
D) Detective Control ✅

> **Explanation:** An IDS **detects** intrusions — it identifies them after
> they occur or during the attempt. It does NOT stop them before they happen.
> A firewall would be Preventive. A backup restore would be Corrective. This
> is one of the most common MCQ traps in security exams.

---

**Q14. A firewall blocking unauthorized traffic before it enters the network
is an example of:**

A) Detective Control
B) Corrective Control
C) Preventive Control ✅
D) Compensating Control

> **Explanation:** A firewall **stops** incidents before they happen — that is
> the definition of a **Preventive Control**. Detective identifies after the
> fact. Corrective fixes damage. Compensating is an alternative when the
> primary control is not feasible.

---

**Q15. When a primary security control cannot be implemented, which type of
control is used as an alternative?**

A) Corrective Control
B) Detective Control
C) Compensating Control ✅
D) Preventive Control

> **Explanation:** **Compensating Controls** are alternatives used when the
> standard control is not feasible or practical. For example, if automated
> access logging is not available, a manual sign-in register compensates for it.

---

**Q16. Which of the following is NOT a recognized type of security audit?**

A) Physical Security Audit
B) Network Security Audit
C) Predictive Audit ✅
D) Application Audit

> **Explanation:** **Predictive Audit** is not a recognized type of security
> audit. Physical Security, Network Security, Application, Technical, Forensic,
> Operational, Compliance, IS, Internal, and External are all valid recognized
> types.

---

**Q17. An auditor reviews server access logs and compares them over a 6-month
period to identify anomalies. This is what type of audit evidence?**

A) Physical Evidence
B) Testimonial Evidence
C) Documentary Evidence
D) Analytical Evidence ✅

> **Explanation:** **Analytical Evidence** involves data comparisons, trend
> analysis, and calculations. Reviewing and comparing logs over time is
> analytical work. Documentary Evidence would be simply reviewing a single
> document. Testimonial is from interviews. Physical is from site observations.

---

**Q18. An auditor visits the data center to physically observe whether server
racks are locked. This is what type of audit evidence?**

A) Physical Evidence ✅
B) Documentary Evidence
C) Analytical Evidence
D) Testimonial Evidence

> **Explanation:** **Physical Evidence** is collected through direct
> observation of tangible items — visiting the server room and observing
> physical locks is a classic example. Interviewing the data center manager
> about locks would be Testimonial.

---

**Q19. During an audit, a staff member verbally explains the access control
process to the auditor. This is what type of audit evidence?**

A) Physical Evidence
B) Documentary Evidence
C) Testimonial Evidence ✅
D) Analytical Evidence

> **Explanation:** **Testimonial Evidence** comes from people — verbal
> statements during interviews or written statements from staff. Physical =
> observation. Documentary = written records. Analytical = data comparison.

---

**Q20. Audit Risk is composed of three components. Which component can the
auditor directly control?**

A) Inherent Risk
B) Control Risk
C) Detection Risk ✅
D) Residual Risk

> **Explanation:** **Detection Risk** is the only component auditors can
> directly control — through better sampling, more thorough procedures, and
> improved testing techniques. Inherent Risk and Control Risk both belong to
> the organization and its environment, not the auditor.

---

**Q21. An audit finding that indicates a serious, systemic failure where a
required control is completely absent is classified as:**

A) Minor Nonconformity
B) Observation
C) Opportunity for Improvement
D) Major Nonconformity ✅

> **Explanation:** **Major Nonconformity** = systemic or complete failure of a
> requirement — the control simply does not exist. **Minor Nonconformity** =
> isolated or partial gap where the control exists but has small failures.
> Observation = potential future risk. OFI = optional suggestion only.

---

**Q22. Which of the following audit findings is optional for the organization
to act upon?**

A) Major Nonconformity
B) Minor Nonconformity
C) Observation
D) Opportunity for Improvement (OFI) ✅

> **Explanation:** An **OFI (Opportunity for Improvement)** is purely a
> suggestion — organizations are not required to act on it. Major and Minor
> Nonconformities require mandatory corrective action. Observations are
> monitored but not immediate action items.

---

**Q23. The correct relationship between Audit Criteria, Audit Evidence, and
Audit Findings is:**

A) Evidence is compared against Findings to produce Criteria
B) Criteria is compared against Findings to produce Evidence
C) Evidence is compared against Criteria to produce Findings ✅
D) Findings are compared against Evidence to validate Criteria

> **Explanation:** The correct flow is: collect **Evidence** → compare it
> against **Criteria** (standards, policies, regulations) → the result is
> **Findings**. You cannot have findings without both evidence and criteria.

---

**Q24. GRC stands for:**

A) General Risk Control
B) Global Regulatory Compliance
C) Governance, Reporting & Controls
D) Governance, Risk & Compliance ✅

> **Explanation:** **GRC = Governance, Risk & Compliance** — a framework that
> integrates all three disciplines to manage organizational integrity. All
> other options are plausible-sounding distractors but are not correct.

---

**Q25. Which of the following is a factor that triggers a security audit?**

A) Increase in employee count
B) Office renovation
C) Post-breach incident review ✅
D) Change in company logo

> **Explanation:** **Incident History** — specifically a security breach — is
> a direct audit decision factor. It triggers a post-breach audit to understand
> what failed. Employee count, office renovation, and branding changes are not
> recognized audit trigger factors.

---

**Q26. The standard that provides guidelines for auditing management systems
and defines audit principles is:**

A) ISO 27001
B) ISO 31000
C) ISO 19011 ✅
D) NIST SP 800-53

> **Explanation:** **ISO 19011** specifically provides guidelines for auditing
> management systems and is the source of the formal audit principles
> (Independence, Objectivity, Integrity, etc.) and the 1st/2nd/3rd party audit
> classification. ISO 27001 = information security management. ISO 31000 =
> risk management. NIST SP 800-53 = security and privacy controls.

---

**Q27. Segregation of Duties (SOD) means:**

A) Physically separating internal and external audit teams
B) Isolating sensitive data in separate network segments
C) Separating the compliance team from the security team
D) Dividing responsibilities so no single person can commit and conceal fraud ✅

> **Explanation:** **SOD** is a fundamental control — it divides tasks so that
> one person cannot complete a sensitive process alone, preventing undetected
> fraud or error. Example: the person who authorizes payments should not also
> process them.

---

**Q28. What is the section of an audit report that contains the organization's
formal response to audit findings?**

A) Executive Summary
B) Conclusion
C) Management Response ✅
D) Recommendations

> **Explanation:** The **Management Response** section is where the
> organization formally responds to findings and presents their remediation
> plan. Recommendations = auditor's suggestions. Executive Summary = high-level
> overview for management. Conclusion = overall audit opinion.

---

**Q29. Which of the following best describes "Scope Creep" in auditing?**

A) Reducing the audit scope to save time
B) Going beyond the defined audit boundaries during fieldwork ✅
C) Adding more auditors to the team mid-audit
D) Extending the audit report deadline

> **Explanation:** **Scope Creep** = going beyond the defined audit scope
> boundaries. It is considered bad practice as it introduces unplanned work,
> may miss the core objectives, and can compromise audit quality. The scope is
> defined in Planning and must be respected throughout.

---

**Q30. Which combination correctly maps audit phases to their key outputs?**

A) Planning → Gap Analysis / Fieldwork → Audit Plan
B) Analysis → Audit Report / Reporting → Evidence Package
C) Planning → Audit Plan / Fieldwork → Evidence / Reporting → Audit Report ✅
D) Fieldwork → Audit Plan / Analysis → Audit Report / Reporting → Evidence

> **Explanation:** The correct mapping is:
> **Planning** produces the **Audit Plan** →
> **Fieldwork** produces the **Evidence Package** →
> **Analysis** produces the **Gap Analysis** →
> **Reporting** produces the **Audit Report** →
> **Follow-up** produces **Remediation Verification**.
> All other options mix up outputs with wrong phases.
