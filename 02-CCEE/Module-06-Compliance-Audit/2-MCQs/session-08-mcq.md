**Q1. HIPAA stands for and was enacted in which year?**

A) Health Information Privacy and Accountability Act — 1999
B) Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — 1996 ✅
C) Health Insurance Privacy and Accessibility Act — 1996
D) Health Information Portability and Accountability Act — 2003

> **Explanation:** **HIPAA = Health Insurance Portability and
> Accountability Act** — enacted on **21 August 1996** and signed
> by President Bill Clinton. The word "portability" refers to health
> insurance portability between jobs — NOT data portability. The
> Privacy Rule came in 2003 but HIPAA itself was enacted in 1996.

---

**Q2. Which US government body is primarily responsible for enforcing
HIPAA compliance?**

A)
SEC — Securities and Exchange Commission

B)
FTC — Federal Trade Commission

C)
OCR — Office for Civil Rights within HHS ✅

D)
DOJ — Department of Justice

> **Explanation:** The **OCR (Office for Civil Rights)** within the
> **Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)** is the primary
> HIPAA enforcement body — it investigates complaints, conducts
> audits, and imposes civil money penalties. The DOJ handles criminal
> HIPAA violations. SEC and FTC have no HIPAA enforcement role.

---

**Q3. Which HIPAA Title contains the Administrative Simplification
provisions most relevant to IT and security professionals?**

A)
Title I — Health Care Access and Portability

B)
Title II — Preventing Health Care Fraud and Administrative
Simplification ✅

C)
Title III — Tax-Related Health Provisions

D)
Title IV — Application of Group Health Plan Requirements

> **Explanation:** **Title II = Administrative Simplification** —
> the most important HIPAA title for IT and security. It contains
> the Privacy Rule, Security Rule, Breach Notification Rule, and
> Enforcement Rule. Title I covers insurance portability between
> jobs. Titles III, IV, and V cover tax, group health plans, and
> revenue provisions respectively.

**Q4. The HIPAA Privacy Rule became effective in which year and
applies to which form of PHI?**

A)
2005 — Electronic PHI (ePHI) only

B)
2003 — All forms of PHI including paper, oral, and electronic ✅

C)
2009 — All forms of PHI

D)
2003 — Electronic PHI (ePHI) only

> **Explanation:** The **Privacy Rule became effective April 14, 2003**
> and applies to **ALL forms of PHI** — paper, oral, and electronic.
> The Security Rule (effective 2005) applies ONLY to ePHI. This
> distinction between Privacy Rule (all PHI) and Security Rule
> (ePHI only) is one of the most tested HIPAA facts.

---

**Q5. The HIPAA Security Rule became effective in which year and
applies to which form of PHI?**

A)
2003 — All forms of PHI

B)
2006 — Electronic PHI only

C)
2009 — All forms of PHI including paper records

D)
2005 — Electronic PHI (ePHI) only ✅

> **Explanation:** The **Security Rule became effective April 21,
> 2005** and applies **ONLY to ePHI (electronic PHI)**. Paper records
> and oral communications are covered by the Privacy Rule — not the
> Security Rule. An organization could have perfect Security Rule
> compliance but still violate HIPAA through improper handling of
> paper records.

---

**Q6. TPO in HIPAA stands for and represents which concept?**

A)
Technical, Physical, Organizational — the three safeguard categories

B)
Treatment, Payment, Operations — permitted uses of PHI that do NOT
require patient authorization ✅

C)
Transfer, Process, Output — data handling stages

D)
Technical, Privacy, Operational — HIPAA compliance pillars

> **Explanation:** **TPO = Treatment, Payment, Operations** — the
> three permitted uses of PHI that do NOT require patient
> authorization. Any use of PHI BEYOND TPO requires explicit written
> patient authorization. Treatment = providing care, Payment = billing,
> Operations = quality assessment, training, accreditation.

---

**Q7. Under HIPAA, the Minimum Necessary Standard requires covered
entities to use only the minimum PHI needed. This standard does
NOT apply to which situation?**

A)
Marketing disclosures to third-party vendors

B)
Disclosures to health insurance companies for payment

C)
Treatment disclosures between healthcare providers ✅

D)
Uses of PHI for healthcare operations activities

> **Explanation:** The **Minimum Necessary Standard does NOT apply
> to treatment disclosures between providers** — doctors and other
> care providers need full information to treat patients effectively.
> Limiting information in treatment contexts could harm patient care.
> The standard DOES apply to payment, operations, and most other
> uses and disclosures.

**Q8. The three categories of safeguards required by the HIPAA
Security Rule are:**

A)
Preventive, Detective, and Corrective Safeguards

B)
Governance, Operational, and Technical Safeguards

C)
Administrative, Physical, and Technical Safeguards ✅

D)
Organizational, Procedural, and Electronic Safeguards

> **Explanation:** The HIPAA Security Rule requires exactly **three
> categories of safeguards: Administrative, Physical, and Technical.**
> Administrative = policies and management. Physical = physical
> access controls. Technical = technology controls like encryption
> and access controls. These three categories and their exact names
> are directly tested in MCQs.

**Q9. In the HIPAA Security Rule, an "Addressable" specification
means:**

A)
The specification is optional — organizations can choose to skip it

B)
The specification must be implemented exactly as described with no
alternatives

C)
The specification must be assessed — implemented if reasonable, or
documented with an equivalent alternative ✅

D)
The specification only applies to organizations with 500+ employees

> **Explanation:** **Addressable ≠ Optional** — this is a critical
> HIPAA concept. Addressable specifications must be **assessed** for
> reasonableness. If reasonable → implement. If not → **document
> why** and implement an equivalent alternative. Organizations
> CANNOT simply ignore addressable specs. Required specifications
> must always be implemented — no exceptions.

---

**Q10. Which of the following is a REQUIRED (not addressable)
specification under the HIPAA Security Rule?**

A)
Encryption of ePHI at rest

B)
Automatic logoff of workstations

C)
Integrity controls for ePHI

D)
Risk analysis and risk management ✅

> **Explanation:** **Risk analysis and risk management are REQUIRED
> specifications** under the Security Rule's Security Management
> Process standard. Encryption of ePHI at rest and automatic logoff
> are **addressable** specifications. Integrity controls are also
> addressable. The distinction between required and addressable specs
> is frequently tested — risk analysis is always required.

**Q11. PHI (Protected Health Information) must relate to which three
aspects of a person's health to qualify as PHI?**

A)
Mental health, physical health, and dental health

B)
Past health condition, present health condition, or future health
condition, provision of healthcare, or payment for healthcare ✅

C)
Medical records, financial records, and insurance records only

D)
Electronic records, paper records, and oral communications only

> **Explanation:** PHI is defined as information that relates to a
> person's **past, present, or future health condition, provision of
> healthcare, or payment for healthcare** AND can identify the
> individual. The "past, present, or future" scope is broad —
> a future insurance claim for a condition not yet diagnosed could
> still be PHI. The last part (A, B, C) describes forms of PHI, not
> the definition.

**Q12. Which of the following is NOT one of the three types of
Covered Entities under HIPAA?**

A)
Healthcare Providers (hospitals, doctors, pharmacies)

B)
Health Plans (insurance companies, Medicare, Medicaid)

C)
Healthcare Software Developers ✅

D)
Healthcare Clearinghouses (claims processors)

> **Explanation:** The **three types of Covered Entities** are:
> Healthcare Providers, Health Plans, and Healthcare Clearinghouses.
> **Healthcare Software Developers** are NOT covered entities —
> they would be **Business Associates** if they handle PHI on behalf
> of a covered entity. Simply developing software does not make a
> company a covered entity.

---

**Q13. A cloud storage company stores electronic health records on
behalf of a hospital. Under HIPAA, the cloud company is classified
as:**

A)
A Covered Entity — Health Plan category

B)
A Covered Entity — Healthcare Provider category

C)
Exempt from HIPAA — cloud providers are not regulated

D)
A Business Associate ✅

> **Explanation:** The cloud storage company is a **Business
> Associate** — it performs a function (data storage) involving PHI
> **on behalf of** the hospital (a covered entity). The hospital must
> have a **Business Associate Agreement (BAA)** with the cloud
> company. Since the Omnibus Rule 2013, Business Associates are
> directly liable for HIPAA compliance — not just contractually
> bound through the BAA.

---

**Q14. The contract that HIPAA requires between a Covered Entity
and a Business Associate is called:**

A)
Data Processing Agreement (DPA)

B)
Service Level Agreement (SLA)

C)
Business Associate Agreement (BAA) ✅

D)
Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

> **Explanation:** A **Business Associate Agreement (BAA)** is the
> HIPAA-required contract between a covered entity and its business
> associates. It specifies how the BA will use and protect PHI,
> breach reporting obligations, and PHI return/destruction at
> contract end. A DPA is the GDPR equivalent. SLAs and NDAs are
> general business contracts — not HIPAA-specific requirements.

---

**Q15. Since which HIPAA regulation update are Business Associates
directly liable for HIPAA compliance — not just through their
contract with the covered entity?**

A)
Privacy Rule 2003

B)
Security Rule 2005

C)
HITECH Act 2009

D)
Omnibus Rule 2013 ✅

> **Explanation:** The **Omnibus Rule (effective March 26, 2013)**
> made Business Associates **directly liable** for HIPAA compliance.
> Before the Omnibus Rule, BAs were only bound through their BAA
> contract. The HITECH Act 2009 required this change but it was
> formally implemented through the Omnibus Rule 2013.

**Q16. For PHI to be fully de-identified under HIPAA's Safe Harbor
method, how many specified identifiers must be removed?**

A)
12 identifiers

B)
15 identifiers

C)
18 identifiers ✅

D)
21 identifiers

> **Explanation:** The HIPAA Safe Harbor de-identification method
> requires removal of exactly **18 specified identifiers** — including
> names, geographic data, dates (except year), phone numbers, SSN,
> email addresses, IP addresses, biometric identifiers, and more.
> ALL 18 must be removed. Removing only some does NOT de-identify
> the data. After removal, the data is no longer PHI and is not
> subject to HIPAA.

---

**Q17. Which of the following is one of the 18 HIPAA identifiers
that must be removed for Safe Harbor de-identification?**

A)
The patient's year of birth

B)
The patient's general health condition category

C)
The patient's IP address ✅

D)
The patient's blood type

> **Explanation:** **IP addresses are one of the 18 HIPAA identifiers**
> — they must be removed under Safe Harbor de-identification. The
> patient's **year** of birth is NOT an identifier (only specific
> dates like exact birth date are). Blood type and general health
> condition categories are not in the 18 identifiers list. This
> question tests detailed knowledge of the identifier list.

**Q18. HIPAA's Breach Notification Rule requires covered entities
to notify affected individuals of a PHI breach within:**

A)
24 hours of discovery

B)
72 hours of discovery

C)
30 days of discovery

D)
60 days of discovery ✅

> **Explanation:** **HIPAA breach notification = 60 days** from
> the date of breach **discovery** — not the date of the breach
> itself. This is significantly different from GDPR's 72-hour rule
> to notify the DPA. The 60-day rule applies to individual
> notification, HHS notification for large breaches (500+), and
> media notification when 500+ individuals in a state are affected.

---

**Q19. Under HIPAA's Breach Notification Rule, when must a covered
entity notify HHS about a breach affecting FEWER than 500
individuals?**

A)
Within 72 hours of discovery

B)
Within 60 days of discovery

C)
Within 60 days after the end of the calendar year ✅

D)
Immediately upon discovery

> **Explanation:** For **small breaches affecting fewer than 500
> individuals**, covered entities must notify HHS **within 60 days
> after the END OF THE CALENDAR YEAR** in which the breach occurred.
> They maintain a log of small breaches throughout the year and
> submit them annually. For large breaches (500+), HHS must be
> notified within 60 days of discovery — same timeline as individual
> notification.

---

**Q20. Under HIPAA's Breach Notification Rule, when is media
notification required?**

A)
For every breach regardless of size

B)
When the breach involves ePHI only

C)
When the breach affects 500 or more individuals in a single state ✅

D)
When the breach involves special categories of health data

> **Explanation:** **Media notification is required when a breach
> affects 500 or more individuals in a single state or jurisdiction.**
> The covered entity must notify prominent local media outlets within
> 60 days. This is in ADDITION to individual and HHS notification —
> not instead of them. The threshold is 500 per state — not 500
> total across all states.

**Q21. The HITECH Act was enacted in which year and as part of
which broader legislation?**

A)
2006 — Healthcare Reform Act

B)
2013 — Affordable Care Act

C)
2009 — American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) ✅

D)
2005 — Health Information Technology Act

> **Explanation:** **HITECH = Health Information Technology for
> Economic and Clinical Health Act** — enacted as part of the
> **American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) on February 17,
> 2009.** HITECH significantly strengthened HIPAA by adding breach
> notification, raising penalties, extending obligations to Business
> Associates, and promoting EHR adoption.

---

**Q22. Which of the following was a key change that HITECH made
to HIPAA penalties?**

A)
Reduced penalties to encourage voluntary compliance

B)
Raised the maximum civil penalty from $25,000 to $1,500,000 per
violation category per year ✅

C)
Removed criminal penalties entirely

D)
Changed penalties from annual to per-incident calculations

> **Explanation:** **HITECH raised the maximum HIPAA civil penalty
> cap from $25,000 to $1,500,000** per violation category per year.
> This dramatic increase — a 60x multiplier — gave HIPAA real
> enforcement teeth. Before HITECH, the low penalties meant
> organizations had little financial incentive to invest heavily in
> HIPAA compliance. Criminal penalties were retained and remain
> up to $250,000 + 10 years imprisonment.

**Q23. The highest tier of HIPAA civil penalties (Tier 4) applies
to which type of violation and carries what maximum annual penalty?**

A)
Did not know — $25,000 maximum

B)
Reasonable cause — $100,000 maximum

C)
Willful neglect corrected within 30 days — $250,000 maximum

D)
Willful neglect NOT corrected within 30 days — $1,500,000 maximum ✅

> **Explanation:** **HIPAA Tier 4 = Willful Neglect — NOT corrected
> within 30 days = maximum $1,500,000 per violation category per
> year.** The four tiers based on culpability are: Tier 1 (didn't
> know) → Tier 2 (reasonable cause) → Tier 3 (willful neglect
> corrected) → Tier 4 (willful neglect uncorrected = worst).
> Higher culpability = higher penalty.

**Q24. The Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP) under HIPAA must be
provided to patients at which point?**

A)
Only when a patient requests it in writing

B)
Only after a data breach has occurred

C)
At the first point of contact or service delivery ✅

D)
Annually — regardless of when the patient first visited

> **Explanation:** The **NPP must be provided at the FIRST point of
> contact or service delivery** — for example, when a patient first
> visits a doctor's office or is admitted to a hospital. It must be
> written in plain language and explain how PHI will be used, patient
> rights, and how to file complaints. Covered entities must make a
> good faith effort to obtain written acknowledgment of receipt.

**Q25. The two methods HIPAA allows for de-identifying PHI are:**

A)
Anonymisation and Pseudonymisation

B)
Encryption and Tokenisation

C)
Safe Harbor and Expert Determination ✅

D)
Redaction and Aggregation

> **Explanation:** HIPAA defines exactly **two de-identification
> methods: Safe Harbor** (remove all 18 specified identifiers) and
> **Expert Determination** (a qualified statistician certifies that
> re-identification risk is very small). Anonymisation and
> pseudonymisation are GDPR concepts. Encryption and tokenisation
> are security techniques — they do NOT de-identify data under HIPAA.

**Q26. A hospital accidentally sends a patient's medical records
to the wrong email address. Under HIPAA's Breach Notification Rule,
how should this be treated?**

A)
Not a breach — email errors are excluded from HIPAA

B)
Presumed a breach unless the hospital can demonstrate low
probability PHI was compromised ✅

C)
Automatically not a breach if corrected within 24 hours

D)
Only a breach if the recipient intentionally reads the records

> **Explanation:** Under HIPAA's **Presumption of Breach** rule —
> any impermissible use or disclosure of PHI is **presumed to be
> a breach** unless the covered entity can demonstrate a **low
> probability** that PHI was compromised based on a four-factor
> risk assessment. The burden of proof is on the covered entity —
> not the other way around. The sender cannot assume it's not a
> breach just because it was accidental.

**Q27. Which HIPAA Rule established procedures for investigations,
hearings, and civil money penalties for HIPAA violations?**

A)
Privacy Rule

B)
Security Rule

C)
Omnibus Rule

D)
Enforcement Rule ✅

> **Explanation:** The **Enforcement Rule (effective March 16, 2006)**
> established the procedures for HIPAA investigations, hearings,
> and civil money penalties (CMPs). It defines how OCR conducts
> investigations, the penalty tiers, and the appeals process.
> The Omnibus Rule 2013 further clarified and expanded penalty
> provisions but the Enforcement Rule was the original framework.

**Q28. How does the HIPAA Privacy Rule differ from the HIPAA
Security Rule in terms of scope?**

A)
Privacy Rule covers ePHI only; Security Rule covers all PHI

B)
Both rules cover all forms of PHI equally

C)
Privacy Rule covers all PHI (paper, oral, electronic); Security Rule
covers ePHI only ✅

D)
Privacy Rule applies to patients; Security Rule applies to providers

> **Explanation:** This is one of the most fundamental HIPAA
> distinctions: **Privacy Rule = ALL PHI** (paper records,
> verbal conversations, electronic records). **Security Rule =
> ePHI ONLY** (electronic PHI). A paper medical record is covered
> by the Privacy Rule but NOT the Security Rule. An electronic
> health record is covered by BOTH rules.

**Q29. Comparing HIPAA and GDPR breach notification requirements,
which statement is correct?**

A)
HIPAA requires notification within 72 hours; GDPR requires 60 days

B)
Both HIPAA and GDPR require notification within 72 hours

C)
HIPAA requires 60 days; GDPR requires 72 hours to notify the DPA ✅

D)
Both require notification within 60 days

> **Explanation:** **HIPAA = 60 days** to notify individuals and HHS.
> **GDPR = 72 hours** to notify the DPA (Data Protection Authority).
> GDPR is significantly stricter on timeline. This comparison is one
> of the most commonly tested cross-framework facts in compliance
> exams. Both numbers must be memorized cold — confusing them is
> a classic MCQ trap.

**Q30. Which combination of HIPAA facts is entirely correct?**

A)
Enacted 2003 / Title I = Administrative Simplification / Privacy
Rule covers ePHI only / BAA required for covered entities only /
OCR part of DOJ

B)
Enacted 1996 / Title II = Administrative Simplification / Privacy
Rule covers all PHI / BAA required for business associates /
OCR part of HHS ✅

C)
Enacted 1996 / Title II = Administrative Simplification / Security
Rule covers all PHI / HITECH enacted 2013 / Max penalty $250,000

D)
Enacted 1996 / Title III = Administrative Simplification / Privacy
Rule effective 2005 / HITECH enacted 2009 / Max penalty $1.5M

> **Explanation:** The fully correct combination is:
> **Enacted 1996** ✅
> **Title II = Administrative Simplification** ✅
> **Privacy Rule covers ALL PHI** (not ePHI only) ✅
> **BAA required for business associates** ✅
> **OCR is part of HHS** (not DOJ) ✅
> Option C fails because Security Rule covers ePHI only (not all PHI)
> and HITECH was enacted in 2009 (not 2013).
> Option D fails because Title III is tax provisions.
